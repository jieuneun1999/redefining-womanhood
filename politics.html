POLITICS

Historically in the Philippine context, the political arena has been a male dominated space but the landscape is shifting as more women take on leadership roles globally. While progress is steady, it is often met with systemic hurdles ranging from cultural biases to a lack of access to campaign funding. Despite these challenges, female representation is no longer just about "having a seat at the table" it is about fundamentally changing the conversation. In the Philippines, the political journey of women is a narrative of reclaimed power moving from pre-colonial equality to a century-long struggle for the vote, and eventually to the highest office in the land. Filipino women evolved from high-status pre-colonial leaders and Babaylans to legal minors under three centuries of restrictive Spanish patriarchal rule. Despite being relegated to the domestic sphere, they played critical roles in the Philippine Revolution by using their homes for espionage and logistics. This spirit of resistance eventually shifted toward organized activism in the early 1900s through groups like the Asociacion Femenista Filipina and Asociacion Femenista Ilongga. Their decades of lobbying culminated in the 1937 plebiscite, where women shattered a constitutional requirement by gathering over 447,000 "Yes" votes, officially securing their right to vote and making the Philippines a pioneer for women's suffrage in Asia.

Following the approval of Act No. 4112 by President Manuel L. Quezon, Filipino women gained the right to vote and hold office, leading to a growing presence in national leadership. The Philippines notably produced Asia’s first female president, Corazon C. Aquino and followed by Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo, alongside influential legislators such as Miriam Defensor-Santiago, Pia Cayetano, and Loren Legarda. Additionally, the Party-list System Act has amplified women's advocacy, with groups like Gabriela consistently securing congressional seats to address gender-specific needs. From 1998 to 2016, the percentage of women elected into public office ranged from 16.1 percent to 21.44 percent, reaching its peak in the 2016 elections. In the 2019 National and Local Elections, only 20.16 percent (8,782) of the candidates were female. The Magna Carta of Women (Republic Act 9710) provides under Section 11 the undertaking of temporary special measures to accelerate the participation and equitable representation of women in all spheres of society particularly in the decision-making and policy-making processes in government and private entities to fully realize their role as agents and beneficiaries of development. It mandates the adoption of temporary special measures for the incremental increase of women in third level positions in the civil service until a fifty-fifty (50-50) gender balance is achieved and at least 40% women in developmental councils. Several studies presented that women in key positions in government have shown that they are as capable and effective as their male counterparts in assuming leadership and decision-making roles. Promoting women’s representation in elected bodies allows them to contribute to development and nation-building as well as ensure that governance leads to equal access to resources and to development results and outcomes for both women and men. It is also indicated that women as legislators represent not only for women but for the improvement of policy to be responsive especially to the marginalized and less privileged individual. Thus, elected women in office alleviate the quality of life and reflect the priorities for all the families, women, and ethnic and racial minorities (Volden , Wiseman, & Wittmer, 2010). Rosenthal (2001) explained that women function much better than men in terms of leadership, conflict resolution styles, participation, and collaboration and women are known to be less hierarchical in work. Thus, women always proved to stay calm even in a very difficult situation and exceptionally listen attentively to the call of peace making. In addition to that, the Institute for Inclusive Security (2009) indicates women being part of peace processes can probably increase the level of peace stability, lessen corruption, and exemplify health care and education in the community.

That is why the necessity in understanding women is utmost significant especially in forecasting the trend in the participation of women in politics since women play an important role in nation building. Hence, the participation of women in politics can’t be taken for granted since year by year the augmentation is conspicuous. Using the Bass Diffusion model as the representation of diffusive adoption, the study then predicts the saturation of women participation in politics with respect to time and provides a basis in program implementation related to gender equality and/or women empowerment.
