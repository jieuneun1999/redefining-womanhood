ECONOMICS

Historically, the Filipina held a central role as an economic manager in the pre-colonial era, exercising significant authority in trade, finance, and agricultural management, underpinned by the legal right to own and inherit property independently. This egalitarian status shifted under Spanish colonial rule, where patriarchal legal frameworks like patria potestas restricted women’s formal rights, effectively pushing them into the informal economy (Herrero, 2019). Despite these institutional barriers, Filipinas demonstrated remarkable resilience by dominating the retail landscape through "sari-sari" stores and market stalls, while simultaneously driving the nation's cottage industries and providing a critical labor force for the burgeoning textile and tobacco sectors. Currently, The modern Filipina’s economic contribution has expanded globally, with Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs) acting as a cornerstone of the Philippine economy. Historically the economic managers of the household, modern women now serve as the primary breadwinners for millions of families through international labor migration. Data from the 2023 Survey of Filipino Overseas Workers show that of the estimated 2.16 million Filipinos who worked abroad between April and September 2023, 1.20 million, or 55.6 percent, were women (Philippine Commission on Women, 2023). Women are the primary drivers of personal remittances, which collectively account for nearly 9% to 10% of the Philippines' GDP. Their earnings sustain national consumption, fund education for the next generation, and stabilize the country’s foreign exchange reserves. Even in domestic work, their labor allows for the functioning of economies in the Middle East, Europe, and neighboring Asian countries. Beyond money, returning Filipinas bring back social remittances wherein new skills, global perspectives and entrepreneurial mindsets that fuel local businesses and community development.

Despite these contributions, the report of Overcoming the Barriers to Women’s Economic Empowerment in the Philippines identifies critical obstacles hindering full labor market integration, including a concentration in low-skill roles, significant wage disparities, and the disproportionate burden of unpaid domestic labor (Buchhave & Belghith, 2024). According to Women and Labor Market Participation, social norms and child-rearing responsibilities further reduce women's workforce participation by up to 22 percentage points, leaving the Philippines with the lowest female labor force participation rate among the ASEAN-6 from 2012–2019. To counter these systemic issues, the government has launched the Trabaho Para sa Bayan (TPB) Plan 2025–2034. In collaboration with partners like the World Bank and the Australian Government’s Investing in Women initiative, this ten-year framework seeks to dismantle gendered barriers, provide childcare alternatives, and foster a more inclusive labor market that ensures equitable compensation and opportunity for all working-age Filipinos (Duran-Pascual, 2025).The Philippines currently leads Asia in gender parity, sustaining roughly 80% parity in labor indicators, with wage equality and estimated earned income driving its sub-index to 79%. These national gains reflect systematic efforts to increase women’s access to decent work, support female entrepreneurs, and advocate for equitable pay across both public and private sectors. A localized manifestation of this progress is evident in the Quezon City Gender and Development Council Office, which, under the leadership of Mayor Josefina Belmonte, has been recognized by the Philippine Commission on Women for implementing comprehensive programs like the Local Learning Hub, Social Hygiene Clinics, and a Protection Centre. A cornerstone of these efforts is the "No Woman Left Behind" program, a Quezon City LGU initiative that provides female Persons Deprived of Liberty (PDLs) with essential access to healthcare, education, livelihood opportunities, and rehabilitation. By prioritizing the unique rights of female PDLs and strengthening community support systems, the program emphasizes gender equality and prepares these women for successful reintegration as self-sufficient, productive members of society, ultimately fostering a culture of acceptance and inclusion.
