EDUCATION

Education in the Philippines has undergone major changes from the pre-colonial period to the present. Before colonization, learning was informal and taught by parents. Girls were trained in sewing and housekeeping only, while boys learned hunting, carpentry, and other physical work outside. Despite these role differences, men and women were generally treated equally, and women could express influence and even take leadership roles. However, this equality declined during the Spanish colonization. During the Spanish period, education became religion-centered and was limited mostly to the elite. Women were taught to be submissive wives, and discrimination based on gender and social status became more evident. This created stereotypes that placed women below men. A major turning point came during the American period when public education was introduced, giving women access to higher education. In the 21st century, laws such as the Philippine Constitution and the Magna Carta of Women of (2009) promote gender equality and prevent discrimination. Recent data from the Philippine Statistics Authority year (2024–2025) even show that Filipina women have higher literacy rates than men, with a rate of 74.1% for women and 67.6% for men, proving that with proper support and equal opportunities, women can achieve great success in education and professional fields. Supporting these local actions, Philippine law provides a strong foundation for women’s education and training. The Magna Carta of Women (Republic Act No. 9710) ensures that women have equal access to scholarships and training. Furthermore, Quezon City’s own Gender and Development (GAD) Code reinforces these rights by requiring the city to provide skills training and leadership programs for women in every barangay.

Since the turn of the century, the educational profile of the Filipina has shifted to reverse common developing-world trends, with women now leading in several key metrics. While 1980 data showed overall male literacy slightly higher at 84% versus 83% for females, younger generations have flipped this gap. Urban females boast a 97.1% literacy rate compared to 96.8% for males, and rural females lead 89.9% to 88%. This female academic advantage is further evidenced by lower dropout rates in 1982–83, where only 2.3% of girls left elementary school and 6% left high school, compared to 3.3% and 9% for boys, respectively. Consequently, women dominated higher education in the late 1970s, making up 54% of tertiary students, 64% of graduate students, and 65% of those in postgraduate programs. However, gender concentration persists in specific fields. Women comprised the vast majority in food and nutrition (92%) and medical sciences (87%), but remained underrepresented in engineering (14%), law (9%), and maritime education (0.9%). To support this continuously developing trajectory in the education sector, the TESDA Women’s Center, the QC Data Science Scholarship, and the DOST Undergraduate Scholarships collectively represent a powerful shift toward breaking gender barriers in high-growth, male-dominated industries.

By offering specialized technical-vocational training, the TESDA Women’s Center empowers women to master complex skills in fields like automotive technology and electronics, transforming what were once considered "non-traditional" roles into accessible career paths. Complementing this, the QC Data Science Scholarship serves as a localized catalyst for the next generation of female leaders by providing students with the advanced analytical tools needed for high-tech careers. Furthermore, the DOST Undergraduate Scholarships provide critical national support for women pursuing degrees in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM), ensuring that financial barriers do not hinder their academic excellence. Together, these programs ensure that Filipino women are not just participants in the modern economy but are technically proficient experts and innovators capable of thriving in the digital and industrial dimension. Historically, the four periods of women’s education highlight a significant shift toward providing equal opportunities and celebrating diversity in learning. Women significantly transformed education by expanding access for girls, promoting gender-inclusive policies, and establishing institutions that supported academic excellence and leadership. They influenced curriculum reforms by integrating values of education, ethics, and social responsibility into learning systems, while also strengthening research standards and professional training in higher education and health education. Globally, women also shaped learner-centered approaches that emphasize child development, independence, and experiential learning. These contributions collectively improved both the structure and philosophy of modern education systems (UNESCO, 1995; UNESCO, 2015; Montessori, 1912). By moving through these stages, society has begun to recognize the true value and capabilities of women, effectively breaking down the restrictive stereotypes of the past. This progress ensures that education serves as a tool for positive change, allowing women to reach their full potential in an equal and inclusive environment.
